The live data above shows where Schiphol stands today. The three scenarios below model 2026–2030 under different outcomes of the cap dispute: government restriction enforced, balanced approach (gradual growth), or full growth to the 500k legal ceiling.

Cap scenario:
Restricted

The Dutch government succeeds in enforcing a reduction to 478,000 movements from 2026, withstanding legal challenges from airlines and US diplomatic pressure. Airlines are forced to accelerate up-gauging to A321XLR and B787 derivatives to maintain revenue within the fixed slot count. Load factors rise to 86.9% by 2030. Terminal capacity reaches its 70M design limit by 2028, pushing on-time performance below 80% by 2030.

Balanced

The 'balanced approach' framework — required under EU and ICAO noise rules — prevails. The legal cap stays at 500,000 while practical operations grow gradually from ~480k to 492k by 2030, guided by existing noise and slot constraints. Moderate gauge increase supplements movement growth. Infrastructure stress rises steadily but remains manageable through 2030.

Growth

Legal challenges by airlines and US diplomatic pressure succeed in blocking the proposed reduction. The 500,000 cap remains intact and Schiphol grows to fill it by 2030, driven by KLM expansion and recovering connecting traffic. More available slots reduce the pressure to up-gauge aggressively, but higher movement volumes drive infrastructure stress and OTP degradation.

The Dispute Over Schiphol's Flight Cap

The current legal maximum at Amsterdam Schiphol is 500,000 movements per year, set under the Dutch Airport Traffic Decree (Luchthavenverkeerbesluit). In 2022 the Dutch government announced a plan to reduce this to 440,000, later revised to 478,000, citing noise and nitrogen concerns for surrounding communities.

That proposal faced sustained legal and diplomatic resistance. In March 2026, the Dutch highest administrative court overturned the 478,000 cap ruling, finding the government had not sufficiently justified the decision or demonstrated it would actually reduce noise pollution. Multiple airlines had launched legal challenges, and the US administration warned that a unilateral reduction could breach bilateral air services agreements.

Meanwhile, Schiphol itself is opposed to the reduction and its own website references 500,000 as the operative cap. In practice, the airport operated at ~478k in 2024 due to noise and slot constraints, not because 478,000 is the legal ceiling.
